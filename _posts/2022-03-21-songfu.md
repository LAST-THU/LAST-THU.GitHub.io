## Current Research Interests
<br/>
Professor Song Fu’s research interest mainly focuses on the modelling of turbulent flows including modelling of transition flows and analysing flow instabilities in attempts to understand flow physics near the wall. 
<br/>
<br/>
His early work on the modelling of turbulent flows with second-moment closures promoted the development of the CFD software industry. Together with Professors Launder and Leschziner, he proposed the so-called “basic” pressure-strain model, satisfying the principle of tensor invariance, which was set by the mainstream code ANSYS FLUENT as the default element in the Reynolds-stress transport models. He applied the realizability condition and Cayley-Hamilton theory in the development of the nonlinear pressure-strain correlation model and nonlinear eddy viscosity model to improve the prediction of flow separations and turbulence anisotropy behaviour.
<br/>
<br/>
Over the last decade, Professor Fu made significant contributions to the study of boundary layer instabilities in an attempt to understand flow transition and drag reduction. He developed a series of tools in Linear Stability Theory, Secondary Instability Theory, Parabolised Stability Equation & Global Stability Theory, enabling him to study complex boundary layer flows with Görtler vortices, crossflow instabilities, attachment-line instability, and thermo-chemical non-equilibrium effects at high Mach numbers. While highlighting the flow physics of these flow instabilities and transitions, Professor Song Fu further developed the k-ω-γ flow-transition model incorporating the characteristic scales of instability modes to provide wide range of capabilities in the prediction of boundary layer transition flows including hypersonic speed. The k-ω-γ transition model was also combined with Hybrid RANS/LES Method (HRLM) to simulate flows not only with significant transition but also with massive flow separation, simultaneously.
<br/>
<br/>
Working together with COMAC, his team helped the aerodynamic design of supercritical wing for the first Chinese mainframe civil jet C919.
